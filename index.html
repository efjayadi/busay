Why we get Motion Sickness

Motion sickness occurs when your brain receives conflicting signals from your inner ears, eyes, and sensory nerves. When you're sitting in a moving car looking at a phone, your inner ear feels the motion (turns, bumps, acceleration), but your eyes see a static screen. This mismatch confuses the brain, leading to nausea.

The moving dots you see behind this text act as an "artificial horizon." By moving in direct response to the vehicle's actual inertia, they provide your eyes with the motion cues your body is feeling. This synchronization significantly reduces the "sensory conflict" that causes discomfort.
